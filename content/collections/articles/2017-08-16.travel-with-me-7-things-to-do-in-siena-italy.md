</a>After exploring the culture and food amidst the hustle and bustle of <a href="https://www.everydayaccountsblog.com/2017/07/travel-with-me-florence-the-tuscan-countryside/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Florence</a>, we were ready to get a taste of more Tuscan countryside. We escaped to the slower-paced Italian town of Siena. While smaller and less frequented than Florence, Siena isn&#8217;t lacking in things to do. In fact, the historic center of Siena is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and has many cultural spots worth visiting. At the same time, a natural calm fills the streets of this stunning Tuscan hill town that forces you to relax and enjoy.</p>
<p>Siena was one of those unexpected, amazing travel surprises. I don&#8217;t even remember how I picked Siena as an addition to our travel itinerary on our European adventure, but we had never heard of it before planning our trip. All we knew is that it was a beautiful town in Tuscany with a rich history and an abundance of sights to see. I&#8217;m so glad that we visited. Based on that visit, here are seven things that I recommend you should do on your trip to Siena.</p>
<h2>1. Stay at the Hotel Athena</h2>
<p>Two words: rooftop views. I&#8217;m not sponsored in any way by this hotel &#8211; I just genuinely loved the view. The customer service and accommodations were pretty great too.</p>
<p>When we first arrived in Siena from Florence, we walked the hilly streets from the train station to thisÂ <a href="http://www.hotelathena.com/en/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">hotel</a> in central Siena. If you are planning to do the same walk, keep in mind that it&#8217;s a long one and may not be doable depending on the luggage you are carrying and your appetite for walking. Once settled into our room, we immediately went to check out the view from the rooftop. It was stunning!</p>

<h2>2. Eat Fresh Pici</h2>
<p>That evening we decided to walk around and look for a place to have dinner.</p>

</a>We arrived at Osteria del Gatto, a cozy, family-run establishment right at 7pm, and the restaurant wasn&#8217;t open yet. When we returned a few minutes later, they were just opening up, and we were the first ones to arrive. In fact, by the time we left around 8, only a couple of other parties were there. It was obvious to the locals that we were Americans based on our dining hours. ðŸ™‚ We had already learned during the previous two months that it is very typical for Europeans to begin dinner at 9pm or later, even on weeknights, but it was especially apparent that night.</p>
<p>The food was fantastic, and we enjoyed visiting briefly with the owner about his restaurant and his life in Siena. We started with a plate of pecorino cheese drizzled with honey, and we each had pasta dishes for our entrees. It was here that I first tried <strong>pici</strong>, a thick, hand-rolled pasta that actually originated in Siena itself. It was my favorite pasta dish during all of our time in Italy! I continued to order it when I saw it on menus throughout the rest of our Italian adventure, but I felt this one was the best &#8211; perhaps because it was my first time trying it!</p>

<h2>3. Pick up the Opa Si Pass</h2>
<p>We had a packed schedule the next day. We purchased the <a href="http://operaduomo.siena.it/en/visit/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Opa Si + Gate of Heaven Pass</a>Â at the ticket office for the Siena Cathedral which gave us access to the many different parts of the Duomo di Siena complex, including a tour of the cathedral rooftop, and allowed us to skip the line at each one. The pass was less than $20 each, and it ended up saving us money because of how many places we visited in our one full day in Siena. It is actually a three-day pass, so it&#8217;s definitely worth purchasing if you get the chance to stay even longer than one day.</p>
<h2>4. Duomo di Siena &amp; the Rooftop Tour</h2>
<p>This drop-dead gorgeous Gothic cathedral was built in the mid-13th century. Its alternating black and white striped color scheme on both the exterior and interior make it unique among the other cathedrals we had seen in Europe. Black and white are the city&#8217;s symbolic colors. The interior is full of fascinating art pieces &#8211; sculptures by Bernini and Michelangelo and many beautiful frescoes and stained glass windows. Floor to ceiling, this place has impressive detail that I could have explored for many more hours than we had time for.</p>

<h2>5. Cripta del Duomo di Siena</h2>
<p>Our next stop was a visit to the crypt under the Cathedral. This &#8220;crypt&#8221; was not actually ever used for burying bodies; it was likely just an entrance into the Cathedral itself before it became filled with debris. Although it was built at the same time as the rest of the Cathedral, it wasn&#8217;t rediscovered until 1999 during routine maintenance. Â Today, the public can enter and enjoy these long-hidden frescoes telling different New Testament stories. Apparently there used to be a series of frescoes telling the Old Testament story, but those have since been destroyed.</p>
