</a>After a whirlwind trip around Ireland, we hopped on a plane and headed to mainland Europe for the rest of our 2.5 month journey. Our first stop on the mainland was Belgium! We booked an Airbnb for four nights in Bruges, and we were originally planning on taking a day trip to explore Brussels. However, plans changed &#8211; we were pretty tired from our Ireland road trip, and we knew we wanted more time in Bruges the moment we arrived.</p>
<p>Bruges is truly a fairytale town. It looks like it came straight out of a storybook. It was so wonderful to have three full days to wander the streets and get a feel for life in this Flemish city.</p>
<h2>Arrival</h2>
<p>We arrived in Bruges late at night after a nearly three hour drive from Galway to Dublin, a flight from Dublin to Brussels, and an hour train ride from Brussels to Bruges. When we arrived, we found out that the buses had stopped running, so we took a long walk form the train station to our Airbnb. We got some help with our route from a few locals, jumping right into our first non-English speaking country experience of this trip. At first we were hesitant to walk this far with our backpacks, but I&#8217;m so glad we did! We were introduced to this magical city during its quietest hours, all lit up at night.</p>

<h3>Chocolate Shops</h3>
<p>Belgium is known for many things, but one of its primary claims to fame is its chocolate. Now, many people who know me know that I hate chocolate, but I still enjoyed popping into the chocolate shops with Daniel because they were so beautiful and their chocolate creations were so interesting to look at! I could never stay in those shops very long because of the smell, but it&#8217;s an experience I didn&#8217;t want to miss.</p>

<h3>The Bottle Shop</h3>
<p>Another Belgium claim to fame is its craft beer selection. Daniel has gotten very interested in the craft beer scene in the U.S., so he was excited to have the Belgian beer experience. We stopped in The Bottle Shop so that he could pick up a few bottles for us to try back at the apartment.</p>

<h2>Day 2</h2>
<p>The next morning we had bread and jam at the apartment for breakfast, and then we headed out for our day.</p>
<h3>Friet Museum</h3>
<p>French fries are yet another food that Belgium is known for doing very well, so we decided to start our day at the Friet Museum. I really enjoyed this museum. It is very informative and has a lot of cool exhibits to look at. Who knew that the history of the french fries could be so intriguing?</p>
<p><img loading="lazy" class="alignnone size-large wp-image-6875" src="http://www.everydayaccountsblog.com/wp-content/uploads/2016/06/2016-01-30-10.25.44-768x1024.jpg" alt="2016-01-30 10.25.44" width="768" height="1024" data-wp-pid="6875" srcset="https://www.everydayaccountsblog.com/wp-content/uploads/2016/06/2016-01-30-10.25.44-768x1024.jpg 768w, https://www.everydayaccountsblog.com/wp-content/uploads/2016/06/2016-01-30-10.25.44-225x300.jpg 225w, https://www.everydayaccountsblog.com/wp-content/uploads/2016/06/2016-01-30-10.25.44-300x400.jpg 300w, https://www.everydayaccountsblog.com/wp-content/uploads/2016/06/2016-01-30-10.25.44-800x1067.jpg 800w, https://www.everydayaccountsblog.com/wp-content/uploads/2016/06/2016-01-30-10.25.44-600x800.jpg 600w, https://www.everydayaccountsblog.com/wp-content/uploads/2016/06/2016-01-30-10.25.44-450x600.jpg 450w, https://www.everydayaccountsblog.com/wp-content/uploads/2016/06/2016-01-30-10.25.44.jpg 1536w" sizes="(max-width: 768px) 100vw, 768px" /><br />
Potatoes were first harvested near Lake Titicaca in Peru. We visited this area last August (read about that trip <a href="http://www.everydayaccountsblog.com/tag/peru/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">here</a>). I found it interesting to see how the history from two places that we have visited across the world from each other has impacted each other. This museum also taught us to eat as many potatoes as we want because they contain many important vitamins and minerals. Don&#8217;t mind if I do!</p>
<p>Fries originated in Belgium, but no one is certain where and why exactly they originated. One theory is that inhabitants near the Meuse River in Belgium caught fish in the river and fried them. During the mid-18th century, the winters were very harsh, so they were unable to catch fish. Instead they fried up potatoes to look like fish, and thus fries were born!</p>
<div id="attachment_6873" style="width: 1034px" class="wp-caption alignnone"><img aria-describedby="caption-attachment-6873" loading="lazy" class="wp-image-6873 size-large" src="http://www.everydayaccountsblog.com/wp-content/uploads/2016/06/2016-01-30-11.00.08-1024x768.jpg" alt="2016-01-30 11.00.08" width="1024" height="768" data-wp-pid="6873" srcset="https://www.everydayaccountsblog.com/wp-content/uploads/2016/06/2016-01-30-11.00.08-1024x768.jpg 1024w, https://www.everydayaccountsblog.com/wp-content/uploads/2016/06/2016-01-30-11.00.08-300x225.jpg 300w, https://www.everydayaccountsblog.com/wp-content/uploads/2016/06/2016-01-30-11.00.08-768x576.jpg 768w, https://www.everydayaccountsblog.com/wp-content/uploads/2016/06/2016-01-30-11.00.08-800x600.jpg 800w, https://www.everydayaccountsblog.com/wp-content/uploads/2016/06/2016-01-30-11.00.08-600x450.jpg 600w, https://www.everydayaccountsblog.com/wp-content/uploads/2016/06/2016-01-30-11.00.08.jpg 2048w" sizes="(max-width: 1024px) 100vw, 1024px" /><p id="caption-attachment-6873" class="wp-caption-text">Learning french fry cooking methods!</p></div>
<p>We also learned another fun fact about the origin of the name French fry: During WWI, some French-speaking Belgian soldiers offered some of these &#8220;chips&#8221; to American soldiers who thought the soldiers were French. And the name has stuck ever since!</p>
<p>There is a cafe that sells french fries at the end of the museum. We stopped and shared an order. They were quite tasty, even at ten in the morning!</p>
